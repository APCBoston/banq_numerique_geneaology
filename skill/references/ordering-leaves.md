# Ordering out-of-sequence photo batches

Users often send leaves photographed out of order, in overlapping batches, or with gaps. Getting the order right matters because the document's value depends on entries sitting in their true register sequence. Here's the method that works.

## Signals, in priority order

1. **The written leaf header.** Most pages have a number in the top corner ("Twenty seventh leaf"). This is the ground truth for leaf order — use it first and let everything else confirm it.
2. **Entry-number sequences.** B, M, and S each climb monotonically through the register. A leaf with B.82–B.85 sits after the leaf ending at B.81 and before the one starting at B.86. Three parallel counters give three independent checks.
3. **Dates.** Entries are broadly chronological. A date out of order is a flag to re-examine either the reading or the placement.
4. **Continuation chains.** A long marriage act often begins at the foot of one leaf and finishes at the top of the next, with the bride's/groom's signatures or the closing formula on the second leaf. Matching a dangling beginning to its continuation locks two leaves together in order.

## Worked logic

Suppose a batch contains leaves with these first/last entries, shuffled:

- Leaf A: … B.71 (concludes), B.72, M.9, S.36, B.73 … (begins B.76)
- Leaf B: B.76 (concludes), S.36-Pelletier, B.77 …

If Leaf A ends mid-entry at "B.76 … du légitime mariage de Joseph …" and Leaf B opens "…Tétreau, cultivateur, et de Marie Dion …", the continuation chain proves A immediately precedes B — even if you couldn't read both headers.

## When numbers collide

If two leaves both seem to carry **S.36**, one reading is wrong or one leaf is misplaced. Find the **firm anchors** — entries whose number *and* date are unambiguous (a clearly written "S.37" two entries later, dated a week after) — and count backward and forward from them. The anchored chain usually exposes which collision member was misread.

## When an entry has no valid number

Occasionally the firm chain leaves a real entry with nowhere to go — e.g., a burial dated 13 June sitting between S.33 (24 May) and S.34 (9 July), with no free integer. The original clerk made a slip (a skipped or duplicated number). **Don't invent a number to paper over it.** Record the entry in its correct chronological/leaf position, give it a heading without a forced sequence number, and add a short note stating the conflict and what the firm sequence is. Genealogists are well served by an honest flag and badly served by a fabricated number that won't match other sources.

## Reconciling across batches

Each new batch can change conclusions you drew earlier — it may complete a hanging entry, fill a gap, or reveal that an earlier renumbering was premature. After inserting a batch, re-walk the B/M/S sequences and the continuation chains end to end, and correct earlier entries if the new evidence demands it. When you report back, say plainly what you changed and why, so the user can trust the running document.
